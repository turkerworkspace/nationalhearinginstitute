Our research library covers OTC hearing technology, age-related hearing loss, speech-in-noise comprehension, and the consumer decisions people face when buying their first hearing device. Every entry references public clinical or regulatory sources.

In short

National Hearing Institute's research covers age-related hearing loss, FDA-regulated OTC hearing aids, speech-in-noise comprehension, the relationship between untreated hearing loss and cognitive decline, and the consumer-protection questions buyers face. Every claim traces to a public primary source — FDA guidance, NIDCD statistics, the WHO World Report on Hearing, or peer-reviewed publications in journals such as The Lancet, JAMA Otolaryngology – Head & Neck Surgery, and Trends in Hearing.

Research focus areas

Age-related hearing loss (presbycusis)

About 1 in 3 U.S. adults aged 65–74 has hearing loss, and nearly half of those 75 and older do, per the NIDCD. We focus on how this gradual change affects daily life and what evidence-informed support looks like.

OTC hearing aid technology

Since the FDA's Final Rule took effect October 17, 2022, U.S. adults 18+ with perceived mild-to-moderate hearing loss can buy regulated OTC hearing aids without a clinic visit. We track product categories, capabilities, and limitations.

Speech-in-noise comprehension

The most common complaint is not "things are quieter" — it's "I can't follow conversations in restaurants." We cover how directional microphones, noise reduction, and multi-channel processing address this.

Untreated hearing loss & cognition

The 2024 Lancet standing Commission on dementia (Livingston et al.) identifies hearing loss as one of the largest potentially modifiable midlife risk factors for dementia. The 2023 ACHIEVE randomised trial (Lin et al.) tested whether a hearing intervention reduces 3-year cognitive decline. We summarise what this evidence does — and does not — show.

Consumer protection & buying risk

Trial periods, return policies, and warranties vary widely. We document what to look for and how to verify a seller's claims before purchase.

Family-facing communication

Family members are often the first to notice hearing changes. We publish education aimed at the people supporting a loved one through a buying decision.

How this research is produced. Our writers review FDA guidance, peer-reviewed studies, public health agency reporting (NIDCD, WHO), and consumer protection sources. See our methodology and editorial standards for how claims are sourced and reviewed.
